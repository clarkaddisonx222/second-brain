### The Five-Movement Liturgical Framework

The February 6 Psalm Festival service was **brilliantly structured** around five movements:

1. **Dwelling in God's Word** (Psalms 1, 119) - teaching, instruction
2. **Dwelling in God as Our Safe Place** (Psalms 23, 31, 139, 143) - refuge, comfort
3. **Dwelling in the Pit of Darkness** (Psalms 58, 59, 60, 102, 130) - lament, imprecation
4. **Dwelling in the Faithful Love of God** (Psalms 1, 8, 103, 116) - thanksgiving, covenant love
5. **Dwelling with God Who Deserves Our Praise** (Psalms 148, 149, 150) - praise, doxology

**Why This Matters:**
- It's not just "happy psalms" vs. "sad psalms" - it's **theological postures**
- **Lament is in the middle**, not at the end - you move **through** darkness toward praise
- This framework could organize the entire Pray150 project liturgically

**Question I'm Pondering:** Should Pray150 be organized this way? Five liturgical "books" instead of the traditional five-book Hebrew structure? Or both?

### What I Learned About Lament

Section III of the festival service didn't shy away from the hard psalms:
- **Silent prayer** before singing
- **Prayer of lament** (spoken)
- **Imprecatory psalms** (58, 59, 60) set to music: "O When Will We See Justice Done?"
- Reference to **Psalm 102** (one of the darkest laments)

**Key Learning:** You don't just **sing** lament - you **create space** for it. Silence matters. Spoken prayer matters. The service gave people permission to bring their darkness to God.

**For Pray150:** How do we help churches use lament psalms **liturgically**, not just as songs in a setlist?

### Historical Connection: The Genevan Psalter

CICW has a video titled **"Psalm Singing and the Genevan Psalter"** (1hr 8min, from 3 months ago, 1.2K views).

**Why this is HUGE:** The Genevan Psalter (1562) was **John Calvin's complete metrical psalter** - all 150 psalms set to congregational tunes. This is the **Reformation model** for what Scott is doing.

**What I Need to Learn:**
- How was it structured?
- What tunes were used?
- How did churches use it liturgically?
- **Why did Reformed churches abandon it?**
- Is anyone recovering it today?

**Hypothesis:** The Genevan Psalter failed not because the idea was bad, but because:
1. Metrical psalters are hard to translate across cultures/languages
2. Congregational singing ability declined
3. Revivalism prioritized "new songs" over historical texts
4. The Psalms were seen as "Old Testament" and less relevant than gospel hymns

If this is true, Scott's approach (modern English, diverse musical styles, theological grounding in Christian prayer) could succeed where the Genevan Psalter ultimately didn't.

**TO DO:** Watch this video ASAP. This is foundational historical research.

### The Multicultural Insight

The closing doxology ("Praise God from Whom All Blessings Flow") was sung in **seven languages**:
- English
- Portuguese
- Korean
- Cherokee
- Mohawk
- Dutch
- German

All to the same tune (Genevan 134/Old Hundredth, Louis Bourgeois, 1551).

**What This Tells Me:** The psalms can be sung by the **global church** in culturally distinct ways while maintaining unity. Same psalm, different cultural expressions.

**For Pray150:** Scott's vision in South Florida (multicultural, multilingual) aligns perfectly with this. The Psalms are already **globally claimed** - we don't have to export "American worship" to other cultures.

### My Theological Questions

Today's research raised several big questions I need to explore:

#### 1. Why are the Psalms "deeply Christological"?
N.T. Wright's central claim. I need to understand:
- How do the Psalms **point to Christ**?
- How did **Jesus use the Psalms** in his own prayer life?
- How do the **New Testament authors** interpret the Psalms?
- What does it mean to pray the Psalms **as Christians** vs. as Jews?

#### 2. How do we handle the "hard psalms"?
The imprecatory psalms (calling down judgment on enemies) raise ethical questions:
- Can Christians pray for God's enemies to be destroyed?
- How do we sing these psalms without becoming vengeful?
- What's the difference between **righteous anger** and **sinful hatred**?

**CICW's approach:** They didn't skip these psalms, but they **framed them liturgically** with silence, spoken prayer, and theological reflection. The psalms weren't just performed - they were **prayed**.

#### 3. What makes a psalm setting "good"?
Not all musical settings are equal. What criteria should we use?
- **Singability** - Can average congregations actually sing it?
- **Textual fidelity** - Does it honor the Hebrew original?
- **Theological clarity** - Does it communicate the psalm's message?
- **Emotional authenticity** - Does it match the psalm's tone?
- **Cultural appropriateness** - Does it fit the worshiping community?

**Observation:** CICW uses settings from multiple composers with different strengths. Some are more accessible (Sandra McCracken), others more traditional (Celtic Psalms), others more indigenous (Wildmans). **Diversity is strength.**

#### 4. How do psalms form us spiritually?
This isn't just about worship planning - it's about **spiritual formation**. If a church sings through all 150 psalms over a year or two, how does that **shape their souls**?

**My Hunch:** 
- They learn to **lament honestly** (not just "be positive")
- They learn to **praise specifically** (God's character, not just "God is good")
- They learn **biblical theology** (embedded in poetry, not just sermons)
- They learn the **full emotional range** of faith (joy, sorrow, anger, hope, despair, trust)

**Research Needed:** Are there studies on this? Has anyone measured the spiritual impact of psalm-singing vs. contemporary worship?
